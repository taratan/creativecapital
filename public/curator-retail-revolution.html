The Curator Economy: A Retail Revolution (Part II of IV)

The Curator Economy: A Retail Revolution

Content and commerce nestle together like peas in a pod.

Today, Curators could choose to monetize their following through commerce â€” indirectly through affiliate marketing links, revenue share based on ad views, or more directly, through brand sponsorships, collaborations, or self-branded merchandise.

These business models leverage the power of the Curator's brand and community, but are loosely coupled in its incentives. We are seeing glimmers of an increasing shift towards direct, more symbiotic business models between curator and commerce, pointing towards the long tail of ecommerce: the Curator as retail brand.

The Independent-Storefront and Popup-Retail Stack

Most publishing platforms today aren't designed to support commerce. Curators could earn by inserting affiliate links in a Youtube video caption, or tagging a brand in their story through sponsorships. The recently released Shopping feature on Instagram points to a future state where content-marries-commerce, but currently listings are limited to a few selected retailers or brands.

If we look towards Chinese social-commerce apps like Douyin or Little Red Book, we see glimmers of what a Curator-led retail experience could be. Verified influencers are able to directly tag products sourced from wholesale sites like Alibaba or Tao Bao, and followers can check out directly on the streaming page.

Ecommerce stores today are able to set up their entire operation without leaving their living room. They could source inventory through wholesale sites (Faire, TaoBao, Alibaba), dropship products to a fulfilment service (Shipbob, ShipHero), set up a digital storefront (Shopify, Stripe), and even outsource their marketing and creative needs by sending products to photography and video services (Soona, Squareshot). However, while key infrastructure for the Curator-owned retail stack exists, it is still piecemeal and not integrated. Most crucially, Curators also need closer ties between their digital storefront and existing distribution and sales channels like Youtube, Instagram, or Tiktok.

Opportunities to build:

  • An integrated, seamless stack for Independent Retail (e.g. a Squarespace + Square for Retail)
  • Content-for-commerce platforms with integrated retail (Example: livestream shopping on a social network)
  • Marketplaces for curators to source from brands
  • Ways for big-brand retailers to launch a wholesale arm to supply Curator-owned retail with inventory

Interesting retail experiments are on the horizon in Curator-owned retail. Startups like Foundation and Zora, for instance, are experimenting with limited edition products that have dynamic pricing based on demand and trends. They could be seen as the next evolution of the StockX model, where early adopters snag coveted products for a lower price â€” in essence, rewarded if their bet on the trend was right.

We might also see more experiments around membership-access to retail in the ecommerce space a la Costco, where paying an annual fee gets you access to exclusive products or a better price (Italic). Of course, in this digital world, the fee might be monetary, or could be based on measurable, digital contributions like reputation or engagement.
