The Curator Economy: Private Social Networks (Part III of IV)

Private Social Networks

As wayfinders for the web, Curators act as bright beacons for digital communities to gather around. They serenade their followers through Instagram posts, chat groups, forums, newsletter subscriptions, pied-pipering those that resonate with their tunes. As new like-minded followers attract more like-minded followers, the community gets to benefit from network effects.

Physical members' clubs like the Soho House or The Wing have worked in similar ways, but were often bound by geography or often, financial status. Micro social networks that are digital-first are much more fluid, dynamic, and global; its members connect and contribute to community, content, and conversations and tend to add and compound value in much faster ways. Some examples of these micro social networks include many of the interest-based subreddits (r/wallstreetbets), facebook groups (mainstreet mamas), and chat groups on Whatsapp, Telegram, and Slack.

Early adopters could be rewarded for helping bootstrap and build a quality community before the benefits of network effects take place.

Moving from the broadcast model to the network model

As identity becomes less geographically bound and more ideologically aligned, it becomes much more enticing to seek out and engage in curated networks with similar interests. I would expand the definition of micro social networks to include:

  • Readers of publications
  • Newsletter subscribers
  • Twitter followers
  • Podcast listeners

These micro social networks might be less visible, and function more in a "broadcast" format (one-to-many conversations) today. However, Curators could turn their audience into a network, where the curated community, as peers, both create and reap great benefits of a network.

With the right "membership stack", Curators could offer some network-based benefits like:

  • Access to a curated community and conversations
  • Exclusive, curated content
  • Incentives for contribution to the network

Provocations around revenue models

Traditional membership clubs monetized through membership or subscription fees, and in many cases, access had to be curated through an application or a referral from an existing member (e.g. Soho House). With the latter, the social credit or curating "the right to belong" was more important and held more significance than the monetary fee itself, pointing to the value of the Curator.

In the digital world, some interesting evolutions of the fee + reputation membership model could include:

Verifiable proof-of-membership

  • Using tokens or digital assets as a way to reveal access to exclusive chat groups or certain parts of a website
  • These tokens could function as an automated, verifiable way to prove that you had the means (financial or social) to access the community

An example here looks at an experiment accessing Spencer Noon's private AMA chat on Telegram with a limited number of tokens.

Patreon and Substack swim in similar waters, but they still cater to the broadcast model vs the network model. There are many interesting models to be built for the "membership stack" for a curated network, extending even beyond features like subscriber-only locked posts.

An example of reputation-based incentives could be Reddit awards, which unlock access to private subreddits like r/lounge.

Some ways of tying reputation or financial incentives to growing communities could include:

  • Auction models for limited seats or subscriptions (the earlier you bought, the cheaper the membership), which could also be traded or exchanged on a secondary market
  • Membership could be tied to a definitive namespace, so the name can be resold or transferred at a higher price if it had certain access permissions or flairs that was earned over time (similar to highly-guilded users in gaming)
  • Membership value could also be awarded based on increased engagement or contribution to community, similar to the peer bonus system used in HR practices
